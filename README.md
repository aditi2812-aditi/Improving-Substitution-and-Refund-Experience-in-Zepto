1. Overview

This case study analyzes critical user experience issues within Zepto, specifically focusing on:

Automatic substitution of unavailable items
Refunds restricted to wallet credits

The objective is to evaluate how these design decisions impact user satisfaction, retention, and revenue, and to propose data-driven product improvements.

2. Problem Statement

Users frequently encounter unexpected substitutions and are often compensated through wallet-based refunds rather than their original payment method. These issues create:

Loss of control over purchase decisions
Perceived unfairness in refund handling
Reduced trust in the platform

As a result, users experience friction in the post-order journey, leading to dissatisfaction and potential churn.

3. Objectives
Quantify the impact of substitution and refund policies on user behavior
Identify key drivers of dissatisfaction and churn
Evaluate business implications in terms of retention and revenue
Propose scalable product solutions aligned with user expectations
4. Methodology
Data Sources:
Primary survey (n = 30 users)
Behavioral modeling and extrapolation
Assumption-based projections for scale
Analytical Approach:
Funnel analysis
Cohort retention modeling
Segmentation analysis
Unit economics estimation
5. Key Findings
5.1 Funnel Drop-Off
76% of users experience substitution
59% report dissatisfaction
42% escalate via complaints/refunds
35% churn before retention stage
5.2 Retention Impact

Users exposed to poor experience show:

~46% lower retention by Day 30
Accelerated drop-off within the first 7 days
5.3 Refund Preference Misalignment
83% of users prefer direct bank refunds
Only 17% accept wallet-based refunds
5.4 User Segmentation
Segment	Churn Risk
New Users	70%
Occasional Users	50%
Frequent Users	20%

New users are significantly more sensitive to friction and more likely to churn.

5.5 Satisfaction Impact
Experience Type	Satisfaction Score
No substitution	4.5
Acceptable substitution	3.8
Poor substitution	2.1

Poor substitutions result in more than a 50% drop in satisfaction.

5.6 Complaint Drivers
42%: Incorrect items (substitution issues)
33%: Refund dissatisfaction
15%: Delivery delays

Approximately 75% of complaints originate from substitution and refund-related issues.

5.7 Business Impact

Estimated daily revenue at risk:

Users: 100,000
AOV: ₹400
Churn: 35%

Revenue at risk = ₹14M/day

6. Theoretical Frameworks Applied
6.1 Jobs-To-Be-Done (JTBD)

Users expect:

Reliable delivery of selected items
Predictable and fair outcomes

Substitution and wallet refunds violate both functional and emotional jobs.

6.2 Loss Aversion (Behavioral Economics)

Users perceive:

Receiving incorrect items as loss
Lack of refund control as additional loss

This amplifies dissatisfaction disproportionately.

6.3 AARRR Funnel Model

Failure occurs between:

Activation (order placed)
Retention (repeat usage)

Post-order experience is the primary breakdown point.

6.4 Cohort Retention Theory

Users experiencing friction form low-retention cohorts, indicating weak product stickiness.

6.5 Principal-Agent Problem
Platform objective: maximize retention
User objective: maximize fairness and control

Wallet-based refunds create incentive misalignment.

6.6 Endowment Effect

Users assign higher value to refunded money they perceive as their own. Restricting its use reduces satisfaction.

6.7 Expectation-Disconfirmation Theory

Satisfaction = Perceived outcome – Expected outcome

Unexpected substitutions create negative disconfirmation.

6.8 Pareto Principle

A small set of issues (substitution + refunds) drives the majority of dissatisfaction.

6.9 Unit Economics

Revenue is directly dependent on retention.
Loss in retention leads to immediate financial impact at scale.

7. Root Cause Analysis

The issue is systemic and arises from:

Lack of user control in substitution decisions
Over-reliance on automated replacement logic
Inflexible refund policy
Misaligned incentives between user and platform
8. Proposed Solutions
8.1 Substitution Control
Allow users to opt-in/opt-out of substitutions
Enable pre-selection of acceptable alternatives
8.2 Flexible Refund System
Offer refund to original payment method
Provide optional wallet refund with incentive
8.3 Real-Time Substitution Confirmation
Notify users before replacing items
Allow quick approval or rejection
8.4 Personalized Substitution Engine
Use past behavior and preferences
Improve substitution relevance
8.5 Smart Refund Routing
Default refund method based on user type
New users → bank refund
Frequent users → wallet option
9. Prioritization

Using the RICE framework:

Highest priority:
Substitution control
Flexible refunds

These features offer high impact with moderate implementation effort.

10. Experimentation Plan
Approach:

A/B testing

Groups:
Control: current system
Treatment: improved system
Metrics:
Retention rate
Repeat purchase rate
Customer satisfaction

12. Strategic Trade-Off
Decision	Benefit	Cost
Wallet refunds	Short-term retention	Long-term trust loss
Auto substitution	Operational efficiency	Loss of user control
13. Final Recommendation

Shift from system-driven automation to user-centric control mechanisms by:

Introducing transparency in substitutions
Providing flexible refund options
Personalizing user experience
14. Conclusion

This case study demonstrates that:

User experience issues directly impact retention and revenue
Current system design prioritizes efficiency over trust
Addressing substitution and refund friction can significantly improve long-term growth
15. Key Takeaways
Post-order experience is critical to retention
User control is a primary driver of satisfaction
Small UX improvements can generate large business impact at scale
